The macro trigger
India's medical devices sector is growing fast — but most of that growth is bypassing the hospitals that need it most
India's medical devices market is expected to reach $50 billion by 2030, driven by rising incomes and growing demand for diagnostics, surgical equipment, and patient monitoring systems. But almost all of that growth is concentrated in large private hospitals in metro cities. The 25,000+ secondary and district hospitals across Tier 2 and 3 India — which serve the majority of the country's population — have limited access to modern medical equipment, partly because global manufacturers don't know how to reach them, and partly because the procurement, financing, and after-sales service models that work in Mumbai don't work in Patna or Raipur.
The advisory lens
What smart market entry looks like in this context
Entering Tier 2 and 3 hospital markets is not about reducing price. Most barriers are structural: hospitals lack dedicated procurement officers, financing options for capital equipment are limited, and after-sales service outside major cities is thin. Companies that succeed don't replicate their metro playbook — they identify a distribution partner with existing hospital relationships, adapt their financing model to allow instalments or lease structures, and build service coverage into the commercial arrangement from day one.
Athaag's view
The Tier 2 hospital market rewards patience and local relationships over marketing spend. One trusted distribution partner with genuine hospital access is worth more than a national sales team without it.

Private Development Investment
Can private capital make rural retail work in India?
In much of rural India, the shop at the end of the village lane is still the main point of access for everything from cooking oil to medicines. These kirana stores serve hundreds of millions of people but operate with almost no formal credit, limited product variety, and no data on what sells. We spent three months mapping rural retail investment opportunities across Rajasthan, MP, and UP — assessing which models have commercially viable unit economics and which interventions would most meaningfully improve incomes for both retailers and the communities they serve.

kirana stores across India — only 3% have access to formal working capital credit · RBI 2025
The vast majority of India's rural retailers finance their inventory through informal moneylenders at 30–40% annual interest. Formalising even a fraction of that credit access would meaningfully improve retailer margins and product availability for rural consumers.
